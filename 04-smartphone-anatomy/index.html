A Globalised Object

A smartphone is not made anywhere. It's made everywhere.

Every modern flagship is a fragile product of dozens of supply chains, each owned by a different country or company. A single chip can pass through six countries before it ships in a finished device.

Chip Fabrication
🇹🇼Taiwan
TSMC fabricates virtually every leading-edge chip in the world. Their 3nm process makes the SoC, the cellular modem, and most peripheral silicon.
Display Panel
🇰🇷South Korea
Samsung Display and LG Display produce the vast majority of high-end OLED panels — including the one on your iPhone.
Memory & Storage
🇰🇷South Korea
Samsung and SK Hynix dominate DRAM and NAND flash. The chips are stacked on top of the SoC at the foundry stage.
Camera Sensors
🇯🇵Japan
Sony makes more than half of the smartphone camera sensors on the planet — including the main sensor in nearly every flagship.
Battery Cells
🇨🇳China
Lithium-ion cells are largely made in China by CATL and ATL, then assembled into battery packs in factories near final assembly.
Final Assembly
🇨🇳China · 🇮🇳India
Foxconn, Pegatron, and Wistron assemble the device. India now produces a growing share of iPhones — recently passing 14% of global output.
Lithium & Cobalt
🇦🇺Australia · 🇨🇩DR Congo
Lithium mostly from Australia and Chile; cobalt for the battery overwhelmingly from the DRC, which produces around 70% of the world's supply.
Software & Design
🇺🇸United States
Apple's hardware and software design teams are based in California. So is the chip architecture team that designed the SoC.
Awareness, Quietly

Your phone has at least 14 sensors.

Most you've never heard of, all of them running in the background. Together, they give the device a constantly-updated model of where it is, how it's moving, who is touching it, and how much light is around.

A Short History

Fifty years of phones — condensed.

From a brick that could only place calls to a slab that fits in your pocket and runs neural networks. The acceleration curve is steeper than almost any other consumer technology in history.

1973
Motorola DynaTAC
First handheld mobile call. 1.1 kg. 30 minutes of talk time. Cost: $4,000 (≈ $25,000 today).
1992
IBM Simon
First "smart" phone. Touchscreen, email, fax. Battery lasted one hour.
1999
Nokia 3210
Mass-market era begins. 160 million units sold. Snake.
2007
iPhone
Multi-touch, capacitive screen, no stylus. The shape every phone now copies.
2010
Galaxy S
Android catches up with quality hardware. Super AMOLED display arrives.
2017
iPhone X
Edge-to-edge OLED. Face ID. The bezel disappears.
2024
On-device AI
3 nm chips, 16-core neural engines, generative models running locally without a server in sight.

Five Generations of Wireless

From a single phone call to holographic streaming — in 50 years.

Each cellular generation has been roughly 100× faster than the last. By 5G, the network can move data at speeds that match a fibre-optic cable — wirelessly, while you walk.

1G
1980s
~2 kbps

Analog voice only. Calls broadcast unencrypted on radio frequencies — anyone with a scanner could listen in.

Digital, encrypted. Brought SMS, picture messaging, and the first ringtone economy. Still in use in some emerging markets.

What It Knows About You

Your phone is continuously aware.

A modern phone collects, processes, or transmits this information — often constantly, in the background, with most apps having default access. None of these is hypothetical: each can be observed in network traffic.

📍

Your location

Updated every few minutes via GPS, Wi-Fi, and cellular triangulation. Apple, Google, and your mobile carrier all maintain location histories of every customer.

🚶

How you move

Step count, walking pace, transport mode (walking / driving / cycling) — passively recorded by the accelerometer + gyroscope, no app needed.

😴

When you sleep

The phone knows when you stop touching it, when you put it on a charger, and how still it stays. From this it infers sleep onset and duration.

👀

What you look at

App usage, screen time per app, scroll velocity, taps per minute, search queries — all logged by the OS and most large platforms.

🎙️

What you say (sometimes)

"Hey Siri" / "OK Google" listens continuously for the wake word locally. Voice goes to servers only after the wake word fires.

📷

What you see

Photo library is scanned (locally) for objects, faces, locations, dates. Every photo is tagged automatically — try searching "beach" in your gallery.

🤝

Who you know

Contacts, call frequency, message volume per person, who you reply to fastest — used by social platforms to suggest connections.

💳

What you buy

Tap-to-pay logs every transaction with merchant + amount. Loyalty apps cross-reference with discount IDs. Ad networks resell to each other.

📡

Where you've been

Wi-Fi networks you've ever connected to, MAC addresses of nearby Bluetooth devices, cell towers within range — a permanent map.

💓

Your vitals

If you wear a smartwatch: heart rate, blood oxygen, ECG patterns, body temperature. Some of this can detect illnesses before you notice them.

Hidden Costs

A smartphone is a geological object as much as a digital one.

Every device represents an unusually concentrated extraction of metals from a global supply chain. The numbers below describe both what's inside one phone and what the global industry consumes annually.

⛏️

Material extracted

~70 kg
Of rock, ore, and waste rock processed to produce the metals in a single 200 gram smartphone — roughly a 350× ratio.
⚡

Manufacturing emissions

~70 kg CO₂e
Embodied carbon per device. Roughly 80% of a phone's lifetime emissions happen before it's even shipped.
💧

Water used per chip

~14,000 L
A modern semiconductor fab consumes water at the rate of a small city. TSMC alone uses about 150,000 tonnes a day.
♻️

E-waste recycled

~17%
Of all electronic waste globally is properly recycled. The rest goes to landfill, informal recycling, or sits in drawers.
📉

Average lifespan

2.7 years
Before replacement. Doubling the average lifespan would roughly halve the industry's annual carbon footprint.
🌍

Annual production

1.2 billion
Smartphones manufactured each year. Combined, more than the population of every country except China and India.
🪙

Gold per phone

~30 mg
In the connectors and PCB plating. Recycling 1 tonne of phones recovers more gold than mining 1 tonne of ore.
🔋

Battery materials

~5 g lithium
Plus cobalt and nickel. The cobalt overwhelmingly comes from one country — the DR Congo — under widely-criticised labour conditions.
