By The Numbers

The ocean is bigger than everything else combined.

Most of our planet is water — and most of that water is somewhere we have never been. The numbers below are the kind that don't fit into the imagination on first encounter.

71%
Of Earth's total surface is ocean. The Pacific alone is larger than every continent put together.

Of the seafloor. We have higher-resolution maps of the Moon, Mars, and Venus than of our own ocean floor.

Five Layers, One Ocean

Each band of depth is its own world.

Marine biologists divide the ocean into vertical zones based on how much sunlight reaches them. Each zone has a recognisable cast of creatures, a different temperature, and a different relationship with the surface.

Zone 01 — Epipelagic

The Sunlight Zone

0 to 200 metres. Nearly all the ocean's photosynthesis happens here. It's also where 90% of marine species we recognise live.

−10 m

Coral reefs and snorkelers

Most of the world's coral reefs sit between 1 and 30 metres deep. Sunlight still penetrates strongly — reds and oranges in your photos start to wash out around 10 metres as red wavelengths get absorbed first.

−40 m

The recreational scuba limit

Below this, nitrogen narcosis ("the rapture of the deep") starts to impair judgment. Without specialised gas mixes and training, 40 metres is where amateur divers stop.

−100 m

Tuna country

Bluefin tuna, marlin, and most large pelagic fish hunt here. Light begins to fade rapidly — only about 1% of surface light reaches 100 metres.

Zone 02 — Mesopelagic

The Twilight Zone

200 to 1,000 metres. Photosynthesis stops. Many creatures here make their own light through bioluminescence — the only light most of them ever see.

−332 m

The deepest free dive in history

In 2012, Herbert Nitsch descended to 253 metres on a single breath. The all-time no-limits record stands at 214 m for a return dive, but Nitsch reached 253 m one-way before blackout. The Burj Khalifa, for context, is 828 m tall.

−600 m

Sperm whales hunt giant squid

Sperm whales routinely dive to 600 metres, and the deepest recorded dive reached 2,250 m. Their massive heads contain the largest brain on Earth — and a sonar system loud enough to stun their prey.

−828 m

If you flipped the Burj Khalifa upside down

The world's tallest building is 828 metres tall. Drop it tip-down into the sea and its base would still be hundreds of metres above the abyss below. We've barely begun.

Zone 03 — Bathypelagic

The Midnight Zone

1,000 to 4,000 metres. No sunlight whatsoever. Pressure crushes at over 100 atmospheres. Most creatures here are black, transparent, or red — colours that vanish in deep water.

−1,000 m

Anglerfish and the bioluminescent dark

The anglerfish dangles a glowing lure to attract prey in absolute darkness. Males of some species are 10× smaller than females — they bite and fuse permanently, surviving as a parasitic appendage.

−3,800 m

The Titanic rests here

The wreck of RMS Titanic sits at 3,800 metres on the floor of the North Atlantic. The pressure is 380 atmospheres — equivalent to a small car balanced on every square inch. The ship is slowly being eaten by iron-loving bacteria.

Zone 04 — Abyssopelagic

The Abyssal Zone

4,000 to 6,000 metres. The average depth of the world's oceans. Vast plains of soft sediment stretch for thousands of kilometres, dotted with hydrothermal vents.

−4,000 m

The average ocean floor

Average depth of all the world's oceans is roughly 3,700 metres. Abyssal plains are the flattest places on the planet — flatter than any prairie, sometimes for 1,000 km without a single rise.

−6,000 m

Ghostly amphipods and translucent fish

Below 6,000 metres, life becomes alien. Most fish are translucent or pale pink. The deepest fish ever recorded — a snailfish — was filmed at 8,336 m.

Zone 05 — Hadalpelagic

The Hadal Zone

Below 6,000 metres. Named after Hades. Found only in the deepest ocean trenches. Pressure here can exceed 1,000 atmospheres — equivalent to 50 fully-loaded jumbo jets stacked on your head.

−8,848 m

Mount Everest could fit, peak-down — and still not touch bottom

Drop the world's tallest mountain into the Mariana Trench, summit pointing down, and its base would still be more than 2 kilometres above the deepest point. The trench is genuinely difficult to comprehend.

−10,935 m

Challenger Deep. The bottom.

The deepest known point in any ocean. Only a handful of humans have ever reached it — including James Cameron in 2012, alone, in a submersible called the Deepsea Challenger. The pressure here is 1,086 times sea-level atmospheric pressure. And yet — there is still life. Microbes, amphipods, even small fish.

A Hundred Years of Sinking on Purpose

How deep have humans actually gone?

Going down is technically harder than going up. The Apollo programme reached the Moon in 1969. The first crewed dive to the deepest point happened nine years earlier — and we've only repeated it a handful of times since.

1934
Bathysphere — Beebe & Barton
−923 m · A steel ball on a cable
William Beebe and Otis Barton crammed into a hollow steel ball winched off Bermuda. The first humans to see the deep ocean alive — they reported colours and creatures no one had ever recorded.
1948
FNRS-2 — Auguste Piccard
−1,380 m · The first untethered submersible
Belgian physicist Piccard cut the cable. The bathyscaphe could descend and ascend on its own using gasoline buoyancy and iron-shot ballast — the design that still defines deep-diving subs today.
1960
Trieste — Walsh & Piccard
−10,916 m · First crewed dive to the bottom
Don Walsh and Jacques Piccard reached Challenger Deep in five hours. They saw a flatfish through the porthole at the bottom — proof that vertebrates could live at any depth. Stayed for 20 minutes, then ascended.
1964
Alvin — Woods Hole
−4,500 m · The workhorse
Built for science, not records. Alvin has made more than 5,000 dives — including the discoveries of black smoker hydrothermal vents (1977) and the wreck of the Titanic (1986).
1995
Kaikō — JAMSTEC
−10,911 m · Robotic return to the bottom
Japan's unmanned ROV reached Challenger Deep in 1995 — the first machine to do so. Lost in 2003 when its tether snapped. Replaced by similar vehicles since.
2012
Deepsea Challenger — James Cameron
−10,908 m · Solo to the bottom
The film director became the first human to reach the deepest point alone. The vehicle was vertical — Cameron lay on his back inside a 1.1-metre steel sphere for the seven-hour round trip.
2019
Limiting Factor — Victor Vescovo
−10,925 m · The first repeat-capable trench sub
The first commercial sub built to dive to any point in any ocean repeatedly. Reached the deepest points of all five oceans in a single year. Brought back footage that included a plastic bag.
Light, Where There Is None

In the dark, life makes its own light.

More than 75% of deep-sea animals can produce their own glow. They use it to hunt, to find mates, to disguise themselves, and to warn off predators. The deep ocean is the largest bioluminescent habitat on Earth.

Anglerfish
−1,000 m · Bathypelagic
A glowing lure on a fishing rod above its mouth. The glow is produced by symbiotic bacteria that live nowhere else.
Vampire Squid
−900 m · Bathypelagic
Doesn't drink blood. Has photophores all over its body that emit blue-green flashes to disorient predators.
Comb Jelly (Ctenophora)
All depths
Doesn't actually glow — but its rows of hair-like cilia diffract light into rainbow shimmers as they beat.
Dragonfish
−1,500 m · Bathypelagic
One of very few fish that can produce red light — invisible to most prey, who only see blue-green.
Atolla Jellyfish
−700 m · Mesopelagic
When attacked, sets off a 'burglar alarm' — concentric rings of blue light to attract a bigger predator to eat the attacker.
Cookiecutter Shark
−1,000 m · Mesopelagic
Glowing belly camouflages it against the surface from below. The dark patch under its jaw mimics a small fish — the lure.
Bioluminescent Plankton
All depths
When disturbed, dinoflagellates flash blue. This is what creates 'glowing' beaches — billions of cells reacting to wave motion.
Firefly Squid
−400 m · Mesopelagic
Each spring, millions migrate to the surface off Japan to spawn — turning bays into rivers of blue light.
Life Without Sunlight

In total darkness, life still finds a way.

Discovered only in 1977, deep-sea hydrothermal vents host entire ecosystems that don't depend on photosynthesis at all. Bacteria there break down hydrogen sulphide for energy — chemosynthesis. Everything else in the ecosystem feeds on those bacteria. It's the only branch of life on Earth that doesn't ultimately trace its energy back to the Sun.

The Black Smokers of the Mid-Atlantic Ridge

Where new seafloor is being made — where the Earth's crust splits and magma rises — superheated water laden with metals shoots up through the rock. When it hits the freezing seawater, the metals precipitate into tall mineral chimneys that look like industrial smokestacks. Some grow 30 cm in a single day.

Around them: tubeworms two metres long, blind shrimp, ghostly white crabs, and clams half a metre across. None of them have eyes — there's nothing to see. They thrive on bacteria that eat sulphur compounds for breakfast.

Many biologists now think life on Earth may have started at vents like these. They're stable, energy-rich, and chemically active — exactly the conditions that prebiotic chemistry seems to need.

Resting Places

Eight ships, still down there.

An estimated 3 million shipwrecks sit on the world's seafloor. A handful are famous, mostly because of how they sank. The depth varies wildly — some sit in shallow tropical water, others in the abyss.

The Great Conveyor

A river inside the ocean — 1,000 years long.

Heat from the equator and salt from polar evaporation drive the world's biggest circulation system: the thermohaline circulation. A single drop of water completes one full loop of this conveyor in about a thousand years. It's what keeps Europe warm and Africa wet.
