On the Same Scale.

Numbers don't help. Saying "the Burj Khalifa is 828 metres" tells you very little. Drawing it next to a person, a blue whale, and the Eiffel Tower — at the same scale — is how the brain actually understands size. Ten categories below. Pick a tab and see for yourself.

Burj Khalifa
828 metres

The tallest building in the world. Construction took six years and 12,000 workers. To prevent the structure from swaying in the wind, the upper sections taper into a needle-like spire designed to "confuse" the wind into not forming a stable vortex.

Useful Lies of Scale

If you shrank things down — here's what would happen.

Famous comparisons that have done a lot of the heavy lifting in popular science writing for the last fifty years. Each one collapses an unimaginable number into a small object you can picture.

If Earth were a marble
…how far away would the Sun be?
116 metres
A basketball-sized Sun, roughly the length of a football pitch away. The Moon would sit just 30 cm from your marble.
If a human cell were a city
…how big would the nucleus be?
A stadium
DNA inside the nucleus would unspool into a thread two metres long — packed into a space barely visible to the naked eye.
If history were a single day
…when did agriculture start?
11:58 PM
Humans only invented farming about 12,000 years ago. The internet shows up in the final 0.005 of a second of the day.
If the ocean were 4 cm deep
…how deep would the Mariana Trench be?
11 cm
A small valley scratched into something mostly already shallow. The deep sea is genuinely a tiny fraction of the planet.
If an atom were a football pitch
…how big would the nucleus be?
A pea
In the centre. Almost everything else would be empty space. You and the chair you're sitting in are 99.999999% nothing.
If the universe were a swimming pool
…how big would the Milky Way be?
A grain of sand
There are roughly two trillion galaxies in the observable universe. Each is its own grain of sand on the bottom of the pool.

Wonders of Scale

A few genuinely strange measurements.

Things that exist on Earth or nearby that the human brain doesn't have a category for. Each of these is, at some level, a measurement that breaks intuition.

Pando
43 ha · 6,000 t
Utah, USA · Oldest organism
A clonal colony of one quaking aspen tree connected by a single root system. Estimated 14,000 years old. Possibly the heaviest organism on Earth.
The Sahara
9.2M km²
North Africa · Largest hot desert
Larger than the United States. Used to be a fertile grassland 6,000 years ago — geological evidence shows it cycles between green and arid every 20,000 years.
Lake Baikal
1,642 m deep
Siberia · Deepest lake
Holds 20% of all the unfrozen freshwater on Earth's surface. 25 million years old — older than most mountain ranges.
Antarctic Ice Sheet
4,776 m thick
Antarctica · Largest single ice mass
Holds 60% of the world's freshwater. If it all melted, sea level would rise 58 m globally.
Mauna Kea
10,210 m base-to-peak
Hawaii · Tallest mountain
Measured from its base on the seafloor, Mauna Kea is taller than Mt. Everest. Most of it is underwater, which is why we call Everest the tallest.
The Mid-Atlantic Ridge
16,000 km long
Atlantic Ocean · Longest mountain range
An undersea mountain range running from the Arctic to the Antarctic. Almost entirely hidden under the ocean. New seafloor is created here every year.
Greenland
2.16M km²
North Atlantic · Largest island
Three times the size of Texas. The map you grew up with has been lying about this — the Mercator projection makes it look the size of Africa, which is in fact 14× larger.
Hyperion (tree)
115.92 m
California · Tallest living thing
A coast redwood, taller than the Statue of Liberty. Its exact location is kept secret to protect it. Roughly 700–800 years old.
