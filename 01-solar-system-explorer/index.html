How Did Any Of This Happen?

The solar system, formed from a collapsing cloud of dust.

4.6 billion years ago, a slowly-rotating cloud of hydrogen, helium, and the debris of older dead stars began collapsing under its own gravity. The story below is what happened next, in roughly chronological order.

4.6 billion years ago
A cloud collapses
A nearby supernova shockwave probably pushed a slowly-spinning cloud of gas and dust past the threshold for gravitational collapse. As it shrank, conservation of angular momentum spun it into a flattened disc.
4.6 billion years ago
The Sun ignites
Hydrogen at the centre of the disc compressed enough for nuclear fusion to start. About 99.86% of all the matter in the cloud ended up as the Sun. Everything else — every planet, moon, asteroid, and comet — formed from the remaining 0.14%.
4.55 billion years ago
Dust grains stick together
In the rotating disc, microscopic grains collided and clumped over millions of years — first into pebbles, then boulders, then planetesimals tens of kilometres wide. Inside the 'frost line', only rocky material survived. Beyond it, ice could too.
4.5 billion years ago
The giants form fast
Beyond the frost line, planetesimals grew massive enough to capture hydrogen and helium directly from the disc. Jupiter and Saturn formed within a few million years — astonishingly fast on planetary timescales. Uranus and Neptune followed.
4.5 billion years ago
Earth is hit by Theia
A Mars-sized body — astronomers call it Theia — slammed into proto-Earth at an oblique angle. The collision vaporised both bodies' outer layers, which then re-condensed in orbit and coalesced into the Moon. Earth's axial tilt and most of its rotational energy came from this single impact.
4.1 billion years ago
Late Heavy Bombardment
For about 200 million years, the inner solar system was pummelled by leftover planetesimals. Most of the craters you can see on the Moon, Mercury, and Mars date from this era. Life on Earth had to wait for it to end.
3.8 billion years ago
Life appears on Earth
The oldest fossil evidence of life dates from this period. Single-celled organisms in shallow seas. They had no competition for the next two billion years. We are direct descendants.
5 billion years from now
The Sun dies
When the Sun exhausts its hydrogen, it will swell into a red giant — large enough to engulf Mercury, Venus, and probably Earth. After about a billion more years it will shed its outer layers as a planetary nebula, leaving behind a slowly-cooling white dwarf the size of Earth.

The Goldilocks Zone

A narrow band where liquid water can exist.

For a planet to host life-as-we-know-it, it needs to orbit at just the right distance from its star — close enough that water doesn't freeze, far enough that it doesn't boil away. Earth sits in the middle of the zone. Mars used to. Venus probably did, billions of years ago.

Inside the zone

Mercury & Venus

Too close to the Sun. Mercury has no atmosphere to retain heat. Venus has so much it cooks the surface to 464 °C — hotter than Mercury despite being further out.

In the zone

Earth & Mars

Earth: liquid water everywhere. Mars: liquid water once flowed here, but the planet's small mass let the atmosphere bleed away over a billion years. Now mostly frozen, with brief seasonal liquid brines.

Outside the zone

The outer planets

All gas or ice giants — far too cold for liquid water on any 'surface'. The interesting candidates for life are their moons: Europa and Enceladus both hide subsurface oceans warmed by tidal flexing, not the Sun.

Where Are They Now?

Two probes, launched in 1977 — still transmitting.

Voyager 1 and Voyager 2 are the most distant human-made objects in the universe. Both crossed into interstellar space in the 2010s. They're powered by slowly-decaying plutonium and are expected to keep transmitting until around 2036, after which they'll drift, silent, forever.

A Risk Calculator

Asteroid impacts, by size — what would happen?

An asteroid's destructive power scales roughly with the cube of its diameter. Small ones happen all the time and burn up harmlessly. Bigger ones are very rare — but each one could end civilisation. NASA tracks all known objects bigger than 140 m.

~10 m
Once a year

Burns up entirely in the upper atmosphere as a bright fireball. No damage on the ground.

Most meteor showers — including the Chelyabinsk fireball (2013), 20 m wide, broke windows in 7,000 buildings.
~50 m
Once every 1,000 years

An airburst with the energy of several Hiroshima bombs. Destroys a small city if over land.

Tunguska (1908): flattened 2,000 km² of Siberian forest. Asteroid never found a crater — exploded mid-air.
~140 m
Once every 30,000 years

Destroys a major metropolitan area. NASA's threshold for a "potentially hazardous object" — anything bigger than this is tracked.

Meteor Crater, Arizona: 50 m asteroid, 50,000 years ago. Crater 1.2 km wide and 170 m deep, still visible.
~1 km
Once every 500,000 years

Continental-scale damage. Dust thrown into the upper atmosphere causes years of cooling and crop failure worldwide.

No recent example. Last one of this size hit roughly 1 million years ago.
~5 km
Once every 20 million years

Mass extinction event. Atmospheric soot blocks sunlight for years; surface freezes; food chains collapse. Civilization ends.

No known example in human history. Several in deep geological record.
~10 km
Once every ~100 million years

Reshapes the biosphere. Most large land animals die. Species recovery takes millions of years.

Chicxulub, 66 million years ago: ended the age of non-avian dinosaurs. Crater 180 km wide, off the Yucatán.
A Field Guide to Stars

The Sun is one type. There are many.

Stars are classified by size, surface temperature, and the stage of their lives. Most are smaller and cooler than the Sun. The really massive ones live fast and die spectacularly — sometimes leaving behind objects so dense that physics gets weird.

When Bodies Align

Two kinds of eclipse — and one cosmic coincidence.

By an extraordinary fluke of geometry, the Sun is roughly 400 times wider than the Moon — and also about 400 times further away. From Earth, they appear nearly identical in size. That's why total solar eclipses look the way they do, and why this perfect ratio won't last forever (the Moon is drifting away).

Solar Eclipse

Moon hides the Sun

When the new Moon passes between Earth and Sun. Total eclipses last only a few minutes and are visible from a narrow path 100–200 km wide. The faint glow around the Moon is the Sun's corona — visible only during totality.

Average frequency: 2–5 solar eclipses per year somewhere on Earth, but only one total eclipse visible from any given spot every ~375 years.

Lunar Eclipse

Earth's shadow swallows the Moon

When the full Moon passes through Earth's shadow. The Moon turns deep red — sunlight bent through Earth's atmosphere reaches it, with blue light scattered out. Same physics as a sunset, just projected onto the Moon.

Visible to half the planet (anywhere on Earth's night side). Each lunar eclipse can last over 3 hours from start to finish.

How We Look

Eight instruments that changed what we know.

From Galileo's hand-cranked refractor in 1609 to a 6-tonne mirror parked 1.5 million km from Earth — telescopes are how we got everything else on this page.

1609

Galileo's refractor

Padua, Italy

A wooden tube, 20× magnification. Galileo used it to discover the four largest moons of Jupiter — first proof that not everything orbits Earth. Got him in trouble with the Inquisition.

37 mm aperture
1845

Leviathan of Parsonstown

Birr, Ireland

Largest telescope on Earth for 75 years. The first instrument to resolve the spiral structure of distant galaxies — though they were thought to be nebulae within our own.

1.83 m mirror
1917

Hooker Telescope

Mt. Wilson, USA

Edwin Hubble used it to prove other galaxies exist beyond ours, then to discover that the universe is expanding. One of the most consequential telescopes ever built.

2.5 m mirror
1990

Hubble Space Telescope

Low Earth Orbit · 540 km up

Above the atmosphere, finally. Took the deepest images of the universe ever — the Hubble Deep Field — proving there are hundreds of billions of galaxies.

2.4 m mirror
1999

VLT (Very Large Telescope)

Paranal, Chile

Four 8.2 m telescopes that can be combined optically into a 'virtual' 16 m mirror. Took the first direct image of an exoplanet.

4 × 8.2 m mirrors
2013

ALMA

Atacama, Chile · 5,000 m

An array of 66 radio dishes at extreme altitude. Imaged a planet forming from dust around a young star — a direct snapshot of solar-system formation.

66 dishes · 16 km baseline
2021

James Webb (JWST)

L2 Lagrange point · 1.5M km from Earth

An infrared space telescope with a folding gold-coated mirror. Sees the universe's first galaxies — light that has been travelling for 13.5 billion years.

6.5 m segmented mirror
~2028

ELT (under construction)

Cerro Armazones, Chile

The Extremely Large Telescope. When it opens, will be 5× more powerful than any current telescope. Designed to directly image Earth-like planets around other stars.

39.3 m mirror · 798 segments
